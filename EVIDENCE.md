## Summary of the question

**Should a Lateral Extra-articular Tenodesis (LET) — or the closely related Anterolateral Ligament Reconstruction (ALLR) — be added to ACL reconstruction (ACLR)?**

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed, the evidence that adding an anterolateral procedure to ACLR reduces graft failure and residual rotatory laxity is now strong and consistent. The landmark STABILITY 1 randomized controlled trial (Getgood et al., AJSM 2020, PMID 31940222) enrolled 618 young patients (aged 14–25, mean 18.9 years) undergoing hamstring autograft ACLR with or without an iliotibial band-based LET. At 2 years, graft rupture was 11% in ACLR-alone vs. 4% in ACLR+LET (RRR 0.67, p<0.001), and composite clinical failure (graft rupture or persistent asymmetric pivot shift) was 40% vs. 25% (RRR 0.38, p<0.0001). No difference in patient-reported outcome scores was found at 2 years, and a companion safety paper (Heard et al., JISAKOS 2023, PMID 36646169) confirmed no increase in serious adverse events, though hardware irritation requiring removal was slightly more common with LET. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses of randomized trials (Bosco et al., AJSM 2024, PMID 38353002; Mao et al., OJSM 2021, PMID 33997075; Lai et al., OJSM 2023, PMID 37025126; Park et al., Arthroscopy 2022, PMID 36567183; Beckers et al., J Exp Orthop 2021, PMID 34383156) all confirm: ACLR combined with a lateral augmentation procedure (LET or ALLR) reduces graft failure by approximately 60–70% and significantly improves pivot-shift outcomes compared to isolated ACLR, with similar functional scores. The SANTI Study Group prospective cohort (Sonnery-Cottet et al., AJSM 2017, PMID 28151693) reached similar conclusions in 502 young pivoting athletes: combined HT+ALLR had a 4% graft failure rate vs. 11% for isolated hamstring and 17% for isolated BPTB at mean 38 months.

The evidence is strongest for the specific population studied in STABILITY 1: **patients aged 14–25, undergoing hamstring autograft ACLR, who meet ≥2 of: (a) grade 2+ pivot shift, (b) returning to pivoting/cutting sports, (c) generalized ligamentous laxity or knee hyperextension >10°**. A subsidiary predictor analysis from STABILITY (Firth et al., AJSM 2022, PMID 35050817) identified younger age, higher posterior tibial slope, high-grade preoperative laxity, and earlier return to sport as independent predictors of graft rupture; LET remained independently protective for both asymmetric pivot shift and graft rupture. Evidence for benefit is also accumulating in older patients (Pettinari/SANTI 2024, PMID 38794893: >3-fold graft failure reduction in patients aged >30 in a matched cohort). Evidence is weaker or more nuanced for female sex as a standalone factor, chronicity, and family history; these are discussed factor-by-factor below.

One important safety note: a 2026 multi-institutional database study (Hamad et al., J Pediatr Soc NA 2026, PMID 41972212) found a 2-fold increased risk of arthrofibrosis after LEAP in adolescents; this is a recognized tradeoff that surgeons weigh when selecting patients for the procedure. The overall conclusion across the literature is that LET/ALLR is beneficial for appropriately selected high-risk patients but should not be applied universally.

## Notes per factor

### Younger age (<25)

The STABILITY 1 RCT (PMID 31940222) specifically enrolled patients aged 14–25 (mean 18.9 years) and demonstrated a 67% relative risk reduction in graft rupture with LET added to hamstring ACLR. The Firth et al. (PMID 35050817) predictor analysis from STABILITY found older age was independently protective: each additional year of age reduced odds of graft rupture by 17% (OR 0.83 per year, 95% CI 0.72–0.96). In the SANTI cohort (Pioger et al., PMID 36255278), patients aged <20 years were at approximately 5.6-fold higher risk of graft rupture compared to those >30 years. A 2026 meta-analysis (Petit et al., PMID 41733021) across 46 studies confirmed the highest hamstring graft failure rates in patients ≤25 (11.1% vs. 5.1% for BPTB in the same age group). The evidence is very strong that age under 25 is the most consistent risk factor, and it is the foundational criterion for the STABILITY trial's enrollment.

### Sex (female vs. male)

The evidence on sex is genuinely mixed, and this factor should not be overweighted in the algorithm. STABILITY 1 enrolled 49% male and 51% female and did not report sex as a significant independent predictor of graft failure in the Firth predictor analysis (PMID 35050817). Webster et al. (PMID 24451111) found that being under 20 years old was the dominant risk factor, with no independent sex effect after adjusting for age and sport. The 2026 Petit meta-analysis (PMID 41733021, 46 studies) found similar graft failure rates in males and females regardless of graft type. In contrast, some registry data and biomechanical studies note higher ACL injury incidence in females but not necessarily higher post-reconstruction failure. The SANTI groups and STABILITY groups generally had balanced sex distributions without sex-specific LET subgroup analyses published to date. Clinical consensus (Getgood editorial PMID 34972553) does not list female sex as a standalone indication for LET. For the patient-education algorithm, sex alone should at most be noted as a neutral or very low-weight factor, with the caveat that the interaction with laxity (females more commonly have higher Beighton scores) may confound the picture.

### High activity level / pivoting/cutting sports

Returning to cutting and pivoting sports (soccer, basketball, football, rugby, skiing, gymnastics — classified as Tegner level 6+ or Level 1–2 sports) was one of the three STABILITY 1 inclusion criteria (PMID 31940222). Webster et al. (PMID 24451111) found that returning to cutting/pivoting sports increased the odds of graft rupture nearly 4-fold (OR 3.9) and contralateral rupture 5-fold. The Rezansoff STABILITY subanalysis (PMID 37270112) found 96% of STABILITY patients played high-risk sports preoperatively; the graft rupture rate in the ACLR-only arm was 11.2% vs. 4.1% in ACLR+LET, with no difference in return-to-sport rate but with LET patients staying in sport longer due to lower re-rupture. This is a high-weight factor in the algorithm.

### Generalized joint hypermobility / laxity (Beighton ≥4)

Generalized ligamentous laxity (GLL) was one of the three STABILITY 1 enrollment criteria; 42% of enrolled patients had GLL, defined as Beighton >4 or knee hyperextension >10°. Firth et al. (PMID 35050817) found preoperative high-grade laxity was an independent predictor of graft rupture (OR 3.27, 95% CI 1.45–7.41). The Getgood editorial (PMID 34972553) explicitly lists GLL (Beighton >4) or knee recurvatum >10° as a criterion for LET indication. However, evidence on whether LET eliminates this risk is nuanced: a 2025 retrospective study of female soccer players (Parmar/Tummala PMID 40180134) found that adding LET in those with Beighton ≥4 produced graft failure rates (4.7%) comparable to those without laxity (3.0%), suggesting LET may successfully normalize outcomes. Kim et al. (PMID 29502168) showed that patients with generalized joint laxity fared worse with hamstring vs. BPTB grafts at 2 and 5 years, reinforcing that the combination of laxity + hamstring graft is particularly high-risk. Evidence from two European registry studies (Sundemo KSSTA 2022, PMID 35676596; Hosseinzadeh EJOST 2022, PMID 36125587) found that GJH alone did not significantly worsen 1-year patient-reported outcomes, but these were shorter follow-ups and did not specifically study LET.

### High-grade pivot shift (grade 2–3)

A grade 2 or higher pivot shift was the most commonly met STABILITY 1 enrollment criterion: 87.9% of enrolled patients had high-grade rotatory laxity at baseline (PMID 31940222). At 2 years, 26.8% of all STABILITY patients had asymmetric pivot shift; LET significantly reduced this (Firth et al. PMID 35050817: OR 0.56 for LET, 95% CI 0.37–0.83). Multiple meta-analyses (Mao et al. PMID 33997075: RR 0.56 for positive pivot shift; Bosco et al. PMID 38353002; Lai et al. PMID 37025126; Park et al. PMID 36567183) confirm LET/ALLR consistently and significantly reduces post-operative pivot-shift positivity. A high-grade preoperative pivot shift is both a clinical indicator of anterolateral rotatory instability and a direct evidence-based indication for lateral augmentation. This is the strongest single factor in the algorithm.

### Hamstring autograft (especially small diameter)

The STABILITY trial exclusively studied hamstring tendon autograft (PMID 31940222), and the Sonnery-Cottet SANTI cohort (PMID 28151693) compared hamstring alone vs. hamstring+ALL vs. BPTB; hamstring alone had the highest failure rate, and the addition of ALL reduced it to the lowest. A 2026 meta-analysis (Petit et al. PMID 41733021) found hamstring autograft failure rates of 11.1% in young patients vs. 5.1% for BPTB. Small graft diameter worsens this: Yamanashi et al. (PMID 31680741) found smaller graft tibial diameter was an independent failure risk in hamstring ACLR. The 5-strand subgroup analysis from STABILITY (Lodhia et al. PMID 36260487) found no difference between 4-strand and 5-strand hamstring autografts in the presence of LET (failure rates were equalized with LET). Taken together: hamstring graft, especially when slim, is the highest-risk configuration, and adding LET is specifically well-evidenced in this context. For patients who have already decided on BPTB or quadriceps tendon, the evidence for adding LET is less direct (STABILITY did not study those grafts), though SANTI data show BPTB alone also fails more than HT+ALLR.

### Increased posterior tibial slope (PTS ≥12°)

PTS was identified as an independent predictor of graft rupture in the STABILITY predictor analysis (Firth et al. PMID 35050817: OR 1.15 per degree increase, 95% CI 1.01–1.32). Clinical consensus now treats PTS ≥12° as a significant risk factor. The LaPrade group (Tollefson editorial PMID 39580122) explicitly recommends lateral augmentation (LET or ALLR) as their preferred management in primary ACLR patients with high PTS when osteotomy is not indicated. A JBJS Reviews update (Quinn et al. PMID 39018384) states PTS >12° is a "substantial risk factor" and supports anterior closing wedge osteotomy in revision cases; in primary cases, lateral augmentation is recommended. Two systematic reviews on slope-reducing osteotomy (Wang et al. PMID 40968761; Bosco et al. PMID 36308547) confirm the clinical significance of high slope and the protective effect of slope correction, but these focus on the osteotomy rather than LET directly. For the patient-education algorithm, high PTS is a moderate-weight factor pointing toward LET discussion.

### Revision ACLR

In revision settings, the failure rate of isolated ACLR without lateral augmentation is substantially higher, and most experts recommend adding a lateral procedure. The SANTI group (Rayes et al. PMID 34898285) compared HT+ALLR vs. BPTB+Lemaire tenodesis in revision ACLR and found both safe and effective, with low failure rates, supporting lateral augmentation in revision cases. The JBJS Reviews update on revision ACLR (Quinn et al. PMID 39018384) lists lateral augmentation as a standard recommendation for revision cases with anterolateral instability. In the SANTI long-term matched cohort (Sonnery-Cottet 2021, PMID 34351825), patients who underwent isolated ACLR were at >5-fold risk of requiring revision surgery at 104 months, suggesting that at least in younger patients, the omission of ALLR substantially increased re-revision risk. The evidence for adding LET/ALLR in revision ACLR is consistent and supports a high weight for this factor.

### Varus knee alignment (bow-legged)

Varus malalignment increases stress on the ACL graft by shifting load medially, effectively pulling the tibia into internal rotation under the reconstructed graft. The Quinn JBJS Reviews update (PMID 39018384) states that varus >5° "exacerbates stress on the ACL graft, increases ACLR failure risks, and should be considered for correction through high tibial osteotomy at the time of revision ACLR." Biomechanical cadaveric work (Imhoff et al. PMID 33270464) showed isolated varus correction increased ATT and that combined varus and slope correction produced the greatest reduction in ACL graft force. The Tollefson editorial (PMID 39580122) also recommends BPTB and lateral augmentation in the setting of high PTS with any degree of coronal malalignment. Direct evidence linking varus to differential LET benefit is limited; the factor primarily warrants surgical consultation. Weight in the algorithm: low–moderate, sufficient to prompt discussion but not a strong stand-alone indicator.

### Genu recurvatum / knee hyperextension >10°

Knee hyperextension greater than 10° on the contralateral side was listed as an equivalent criterion to Beighton ≥4 for enrollment in STABILITY 1 (PMID 31940222) and is explicitly cited by Getgood's editorial (PMID 34972553) as an indication for LET. The Firth predictor analysis (PMID 35050817) included preoperative contralateral hyperextension as a covariate but it did not emerge as a statistically independent predictor of graft rupture (as opposed to the composite laxity variable). The clinical rationale is that hyperextension indicates lax posterior structures and likely reduced secondary restraints to rotatory instability, placing more stress on the ACL graft. Weight in algorithm: moderate, primarily as a component of the generalized laxity criterion.

### Meniscal injury or prior meniscectomy

Meniscal status is important in two related ways. First, a lateral meniscal tear or prior lateral meniscectomy removes a secondary stabilizer to anterolateral rotatory instability, increasing pivot-shift severity. Second, SANTI data show that ALLR helps protect concomitant medial meniscal repairs from failure: Sonnery-Cottet 2018 (PMID 29741400) found >2-fold higher meniscal repair survival with ACLR+ALLR vs. isolated ACLR (HR 0.443, p=0.033). El Helou et al. (PMID 36734511) confirmed that additional ALLR reduced bucket-handle medial meniscal repair failure rates significantly. The Pettinari SANTI study (PMID 38794893) of patients >30 years also found significantly higher secondary meniscectomy rates in the no-LEAP group (5.6% vs. 2.2%, p=0.003). The Firth et al. predictor analysis from STABILITY included meniscal repair/excision as a covariate; it did not achieve independent significance for graft rupture in multivariate analysis, but statistical power may have been limited for this subgroup. Weight in algorithm: moderate — especially relevant when a meniscal repair is planned concomitantly.

### Prior contralateral ACL injury / family history

Webster et al. (PMID 24451111) found that a positive family history doubled the odds of both graft rupture (OR 2.0) and contralateral ACL injury. The Sward review (PMID 20062970) confirmed return to high-level activity as the most important risk factor for contralateral ACL injury, with inconclusive evidence for family history as an independent factor. These factors are not direct indications for LET in any published guideline, but they elevate the overall probability of future ACL injury events and may inform a surgeon's global risk assessment. In the algorithm these should be weighted low as LET-specific factors but noted as general risk elevation.

### Chronicity (time from injury to surgery)

Na et al. (PMID 34541008) performed a meta-analysis including a subgroup analysis by time from injury to surgery (≤12 months vs. >12 months) and found that combined ACLR+AEAP led to improved pivot-shift grades and graft failure rates **regardless of chronicity**, suggesting that delayed surgery does not by itself eliminate the benefit of adding a lateral procedure. This factor has low direct impact on the LET recommendation direction but longer chronicity may be associated with greater secondary stabilizer attrition (lateral meniscal wear, capsular stretching) that worsens rotatory instability, reinforcing the potential benefit of LET. Weight in algorithm: low as a stand-alone factor.
